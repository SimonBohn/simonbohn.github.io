I'm Simon Bohn, a neuroscientist at the University of Pennsylvania. I study how activity in ensembles of neurons build complex experiences like seeing and remembering. I am particularly interested in how single groups of neurons simultaneously support multiple behaviors. 

# From seeing to remembering
How does seeing a picture become the memory that it has been seen? I am currently investigating this question in Nicole Rust's lab, where I recorded from populations of neurons in the ventral visual stream and the hippocampus and uncovered evidence for a previously unknown computation transforming the neural representation between these two brain areas. This work answered long standing questions about the neural code for familiarity and the role of medial temporal lobe in visual memory.

 Click here for more detail on this project and [here](https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.1101/2025.06.13.659490v3) for the full paper. 

#  From neural activity to seeing
How does neural activity in the ventral visual stream lead to the perception of seeing? In Arash Afraz's lab at the National Institutes of Health, I used optogenetics to map the relationship between neural activity and perception. I found that this method could reliably elicit conscious percepts from stimulations in ventral visual cortex, and that the ability to detect these percepts varied with what was being concurrently viewed. These findings have implications in the development of visual prostheses, as well as furthering our understanding of how neural activity is read out to generate visual perception.
